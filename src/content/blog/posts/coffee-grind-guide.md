Here is a fact most coffee drinkers do not know: **the grind is often more important than the bean.** You can start with exceptional single-origin coffee — grown in volcanic soil, roasted with care, shipped fresh — and ruin it entirely with the wrong grind size. Over-extract with a grind that is too fine and you get bitterness. Under-extract with one that is too coarse and you get sour, weak, flat coffee.

## Why grind size matters so much

Coffee extraction is a chemistry problem. When hot water passes through ground coffee, it dissolves hundreds of compounds — acids, sugars, oils, bitter elements — in a specific sequence. The grind size controls how quickly that extraction happens.

- **Too coarse:** water moves through too fast, extracts the easy stuff first (sour acids), and leaves the complex flavours behind. Result: thin, watery, acidic.
- **Too fine:** water moves through too slowly, over-extracts, and pulls harsh bitter compounds. Result: astringent, harsh, unpleasant.
- **Just right:** full extraction within the correct brew time for your method. Result: balanced, complex, clean.

Every brew method has a specific contact time between water and coffee. A French press steeps for 3–4 minutes; an espresso machine extracts in 25–30 seconds. Those wildly different timeframes require completely different grind sizes. Using espresso grind in a French press, for example, will give you an over-extracted, sludgy, bitter mess.

## The 8 grind options: what each one is for

### Whole bean — not ground; grind fresh at home

**Best for:** any brew method — grind fresh before brewing.

Whole bean is the freshest option. Coffee starts losing flavour compounds within 20–30 minutes of being ground, as the volatile aromatic oils oxidise. If you have a grinder at home, whole bean will always produce a better cup — no matter which brew method you use. Grind just before you brew, and grind only as much as you need.

### French press — coarse; large, irregular particles, like rough sea salt

**Best for:** French press, cold brew.

French press uses full immersion brewing — the grounds sit in water for 3–4 minutes before you press the plunger. That long contact time requires a coarse grind so you do not over-extract. The coarse grind also stays above the metal filter rather than passing through it. French press produces a full-bodied, rich cup with natural oils intact — no paper filter to absorb them. Our [Antigua Ember (Guatemala)](/origins/guatemala) and [Sumatra Black (Indonesia)](/origins/indonesia) both shine in a French press.

### Percolator — coarse-medium; slightly finer than French press

**Best for:** stovetop percolator, electric percolator.

Percolators continuously cycle boiling water through the grounds, which means the coffee is exposed to heat and water for an extended period. A coarse-medium grind prevents over-extraction during that repeated cycling. Percolators fell out of fashion with drip machines but have a loyal following for their bold, strong brew. Note: because water temperature in a percolator often exceeds the ideal 90–96°C range, a slightly coarser grind can help balance out any bitterness.

### Drip (flat bottom filter) — medium; like coarse sand

**Best for:** auto-drip machines with flat-bottom basket filters.

The most common home brewer in Canada. Flat-bottom basket filters have a wider surface area, which means water spreads evenly across the grounds and drains at a moderate pace. A medium grind matches that flow rate for balanced extraction. If your drip coffee tastes bitter, try a slightly coarser grind. If it tastes weak and sour, go slightly finer. The sweet spot for most drip machines is in the centre of the medium range.

### Pour over (cone filter) — medium-fine; slightly finer than drip, like table salt

**Best for:** V60, Chemex, Kalita Wave, cone-style drippers.

Pour-over cone filters have a narrow bottom opening that slows water flow compared to a flat-bottom basket. That slower drain rate means you need a slightly finer grind to match the extended contact time. Pour over is one of the most flavour-transparent methods — the paper filter removes most oils and fines, producing a clean, bright cup that shows off origin character beautifully. Our [Primera Luz (Costa Rica)](/origins/costa-rica) and [Inca Ascent (Peru)](/origins/peru) are excellent pour-over choices.

### AeroPress — medium-fine (versatile — experiment)

**Best for:** AeroPress, AeroPress Go.

The AeroPress is the most forgiving and flexible brewer on this list. Because you control steep time and pressure manually, it works with a wide range of grind sizes — from medium all the way to fine, depending on the recipe and your preferred cup style. Many AeroPress enthusiasts use a medium-fine grind as their baseline. If you like a more concentrated, espresso-style result, go finer. If you prefer something closer to pour over, go medium. It is genuinely worth experimenting.

### Espresso machine — fine; like fine table salt, almost powdery

**Best for:** home espresso machines, stovetop moka pot.

Espresso forces pressurised water through tightly packed grounds in 25–30 seconds. That very short contact time requires a very fine grind to create enough resistance for proper extraction. Getting espresso grind right is the most technical of all the methods — a difference of half a notch on your grinder can mean the difference between under-extracted (sour, blond) and over-extracted (dark, bitter) espresso. Our [Sumatra Black](/origins/indonesia) dark roast is a natural fit for espresso, producing a rich, full-bodied shot with crema.

### Turkish — extra fine; almost a powder, finer than espresso

**Best for:** Turkish coffee, Greek coffee (ibrik/cezve).

Turkish coffee is prepared by simmering extra-fine ground coffee directly in water — unfiltered — in a small copper or brass pot called a cezve or ibrik. The grounds are never filtered out; they settle at the bottom of the cup. This method requires the finest grind of all — finer than you can achieve with most home grinders. If you want Turkish grind, use a dedicated Turkish grinder or ask for it when you order (we grind to this spec on request). The result is intensely flavoured, thick, and aromatic.

## So which grind is "best"?

The honest answer: **whichever one matches your brewer.** There is no universally superior grind — only the right grind for the right method. That said, a few things are worth knowing:

- **Whole bean** is always the freshest option if you have a grinder. The flavour difference is real and noticeable, especially in lighter roasts where delicate aromatics are easier to lose.
- **Pour over** tends to produce the clearest expression of a coffee’s origin character — ideal if you are buying single-origin coffee because you actually want to taste where it came from.
- **French press** gives you the most full-bodied cup — great for dark roasts like Sumatra Black, where you want richness and depth over brightness.
- **Espresso** concentrates everything — the good and the bad. High-quality single-origin beans make exceptional espresso; commodity blends tend to taste flat and harsh under pressure.

The worst thing you can do is use a pre-ground "universal grind" bag on a specialised brewer. It is a compromise that tends to serve nobody well.
