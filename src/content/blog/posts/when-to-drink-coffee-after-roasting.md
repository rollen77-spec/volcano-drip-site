## Why you shouldn't brew coffee immediately after roasting

When coffee is roasted, a large amount of carbon dioxide (CO₂) gets trapped inside the bean as a by-product of the roasting process. For the first few days after roasting, those beans are actively releasing that CO₂ — a process called degassing.

> "Think of it like a freshly baked loaf of bread — it needs to cool and settle." — Bill, Head Roaster, Volcano Drip

Brew during that degassing window and something called **channeling** happens. The escaping CO₂ interferes with how water moves through the coffee grounds. Instead of saturating evenly, the water finds paths of least resistance — little channels through the coffee bed — which means some grounds get over-extracted and bitter while others are under-extracted and sour. You're not tasting the bean. You're tasting the chaos.

You might have seen this in a pour over: that aggressive bloom when you pour the first water. That's CO₂ escaping in real time. On beans that are still actively degassing, it's so forceful it disrupts the whole brew.

Those one-way valves you see on specialty coffee bags? Their entire purpose is to let that CO₂ escape without letting oxygen — the actual enemy of freshness — get in. They're not decorative. They're functional.

## The sweet spot: when to start brewing

The right rest period depends on your brew method — and this is one of the most practical things Bill covers in the podcast. The numbers are more specific than most people expect:

- **Filter coffee and pour over:** start on day 3–5 after roasting. Day 4 is a reliable baseline. By then, enough CO₂ has dissipated that you'll get an even extraction.
- **Espresso:** wait longer — minimum 7 days, but many espressos don't hit their peak until day 10, 12, or even 14 post-roast. Espresso is a high-pressure, concentrated extraction method. It's extremely sensitive to excess gas. Rushing it gives you a blond, sour, under-developed shot.
- **Cold brew:** because cold brew uses time rather than heat and pressure, it's more forgiving — day 5 or 6 is typically fine.

Once the CO₂ has settled, what you get is the actual flavour the roaster worked to develop. The sharp, roasty notes from the Maillard reaction mellow out. The more delicate aromatic compounds — the esters and aldehydes responsible for fruity, floral, and chocolatey notes — come to the forefront. The coffee becomes, as Bill puts it, more balanced, cohesive, and ultimately more delicious.

> "The roaster worked so hard to develop these flavours — a little rest allows them to be properly perceived." — Bill

## The peak freshness window

After the rest period ends, you're in the peak freshness window — typically days 3–28 after roasting, depending on roast level and brew method. This is when the coffee is at its most expressive.

For our single-origin volcanic roasts, this is when the terroir is most audible in the cup. The citrus brightness of our [Primera Luz (Costa Rica)](/origins/costa-rica), the caramel depth of the [Antigua Ember (Guatemala)](/origins/guatemala), the earthy smokiness of the [Sumatra Black (Indonesia)](/origins/indonesia) — these notes are most vivid during peak freshness. After day 28, they're still there, but softer.

Bill's practical advice from the podcast: brew a cup on day 4, another on day 7, and another on day 10, with the same beans. Take notes. The evolution you taste will teach you more about that specific bean than anything written on the bag — and it'll help you find your own personal sweet spot.

## What happens when coffee goes stale

The degassing window closes around day 30–45 in a properly sealed bag. Once the CO₂ is gone, a different process takes over: **oxidation**. This is where most supermarket coffee lives permanently.

Oxidation is what happens when oxygen interacts with the natural oils in the coffee bean — oils that carry most of the aroma and flavour. Those oils go rancid. The volatile aromatic compounds that hit you when you first open a fresh bag evaporate over time, and what's left behind is flat, woody, papery, and generically bitter. The unique character of the origin disappears.

Notice that supermarket coffee rarely has a "roasted on" date — only a "best by" date, often months away. As Bill points out in the podcast: that best-by date is purely about shelf stability. It has nothing to do with peak flavour. By the time that coffee reaches your cup, it's likely been stale for weeks.

> "If it doesn't have a roasted-on date, walk away." — Bill

## Pre-ground coffee: why freshness matters even more

Grinding the bean increases its surface area by roughly 10,000 times — which means it's exposed to far more oxygen, and the staling process accelerates dramatically. Pre-ground coffee kept in an open bag can go noticeably flat within 15–20 minutes of grinding.

This is why grinding right before you brew is, as Bill calls it, the single biggest upgrade anyone can make — right after buying fresh beans. If you're buying single-origin coffee because you want to taste the origin, grinding fresh is the step that makes that possible.

## How to protect your freshness window

- **Airtight container:** essential once the bag is open. Exposure to open air is the fastest route to stale.
- **Dark and cool:** UV degrades aromatic compounds. A cupboard beats a clear jar on a bright counter.
- **Away from heat:** never store beans above a coffee machine or near the stove.
- **Skip the fridge for everyday use:** the condensation from temperature cycling introduces moisture. Freeze only if storing a large batch long-term — and use a proper airtight, moisture-proof bag.
- **Grind fresh:** buy whole bean and grind just before brewing if at all possible.
